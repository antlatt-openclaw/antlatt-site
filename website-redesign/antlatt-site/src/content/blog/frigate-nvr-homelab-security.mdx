Your security cameras shouldn't phone home to a stranger's server. Every Ring doorbell, every Nest camera, every Arlo hub sends your video to the cloud—where employees can peek, law enforcement can request access, and data breaches can expose your most private moments.

Frigate NVR flips that model entirely. All processing happens on your hardware. Your footage stays on your network. Your privacy remains in your control.

In 2026, Frigate has become the go-to choice for homelabbers who want enterprise-grade security features without the privacy compromises of cloud cameras. Let's build one.

## What Makes Frigate Different

Traditional NVRs use simple motion detection. A shadow moves across your driveway? Alert. A tree sways in the wind? Alert. A bug flies past the lens? Alert. Your phone becomes a noise machine.

Frigate uses real-time AI object detection. It doesn't just see "motion"—it identifies people, cars, dogs, cats, and dozens of other objects. The result is dramatically fewer false alerts and genuinely useful notifications.

### Key Features in 2026

The project has matured rapidly. Recent versions have added features that were previously locked behind subscriptions:

- **Face Recognition** — Identify family members and frequent visitors
- **License Plate Recognition** — Track vehicles entering your property
- **Native WebPush Notifications** — No third-party services required
- **Explore Pane** — Filter and search through detected objects
- **AI-Generated Summaries** — Quick overviews of daily activity

:::tip[What About Frigate+?]
Frigate offers an optional paid subscription called Frigate+ that provides custom-trained AI models tuned specifically for security camera footage. It's entirely optional—the free version includes all core features including face recognition and license plate detection.
:::

## Hardware Requirements: The Honest Truth

Frigate needs real compute resources. This isn't a lightweight container you can tuck onto an already-buried Raspberry Pi. Let's break down what you actually need.

### Minimum vs Recommended

| Component | Minimum | Recommended | High-End |
|-----------|---------|-------------|----------|
| RAM | 4GB | 8-16GB | 16GB+ |
| CPU | Modern multi-core | Intel i5/i7 with Quick Sync | Multi-core server CPU |
| Storage | SSD preferred | SSD + HDD archive | Enterprise SSD/NVR HDD |
| Network | Gigabit | Gigabit | 10GbE for large setups |

:::caution[Mind Your Memory]
4GB works for basic setups with a dedicated AI accelerator and no enrichment features. 8GB is required for face recognition and license plate detection. 16GB is recommended for 8+ cameras or heavy use of enrichment features.
:::

### AI Acceleration: Your Most Important Decision

This is where Frigate lives or dies. You have several options, and the landscape has shifted considerably in 2026.

#### Google Coral Edge TPU

The original Frigate accelerator. Cheap (~$60), extremely power efficient (~2W), and still widely used.

**Status:** Officially "no longer recommended for new installations" per Frigate documentation.

**Why the demotion:** Newer options offer better performance-per-dollar, and supply issues have plagued Coral availability. It still works well for existing setups, but consider alternatives for new builds.

**Best for:** Existing installations, ultra-low-power systems, Raspberry Pi deployments

#### NVIDIA GPUs

The current performance king. An RTX 4060 can achieve ~6ms detection latency with TensorRT optimization.

**Requirements:** CUDA 12.x support, driver 545+, Compute Capability 5.0+ (Maxwell architecture or newer—basically any GPU from 2014 onwards).

**Bonus:** NVIDIA GPUs can also handle video decoding, offloading work from your CPU.

**Best for:** Large installations (12+ cameras), users wanting maximum performance, setups already equipped with NVIDIA hardware

#### Intel GPUs (OpenVINO)

Often overlooked, but Intel's integrated GPUs are excellent for AI acceleration. If you're already building on Intel, this is essentially free acceleration.

**Options:** 6th Gen Intel Core or newer (Skylake and later), Intel Arc discrete GPUs

**Best for:** Budget builds, Intel-based systems, low-power setups

#### Hailo-8 / Hailo-8L

The new kid on the block. PCIe-based AI accelerators that automatically download and use appropriate YOLOv6 models.

**Best for:** New installations wanting dedicated AI hardware without GPU power draw

### What I Recommend

:::tip[My Hardware Picks]
**Budget / Low Power:** Intel N100 mini PC with integrated GPU OpenVINO detection. Total cost: ~$200, power: 10-15W idle.

**Mid-Range:** Used Dell Optiplex with i5-8500 (6th gen Intel, excellent QuickSync + OpenVINO). Total cost: ~$250, power: 25-40W.

**Performance:** Mini PC or custom build with NVIDIA GPU. Total cost: $500-800, power: 60-100W.
:::

### Camera Requirements

Your cameras need to speak RTSP—that's the only hard requirement. Beyond that:

**Essential:**
- RTSP streaming support
- H.264 video codec (H.265 has limited support)
- AAC audio if you want sound

**Highly Recommended:**
- **Substream support** — Use a low-resolution stream for detection and high-resolution for recording. This dramatically reduces CPU load.
- **Local recording to SD card** — Redundant backup if your NVR goes offline

**Resolution Strategy:**
- Detection stream: 720p-1080p (AI detection doesn't need 4K)
- Recording stream: Whatever your camera supports (4K is fine)

The substream approach is critical. Feeding 4K video to AI detection is wasteful—720p is plenty for detecting people and cars, and it reduces CPU/GPU load by 75% or more.

## Setting Up Frigate

Let's walk through a complete installation using Docker—the most flexible approach for homelabbers.

### Step 1: Create the Directory Structure

```bash
mkdir -p /opt/frigate/config
mkdir -p /opt/frigate/media
```

### Step 2: Create docker-compose.yml

```yaml
version: "3.9"
services:
  frigate:
    container_name: frigate
    privileged: true
    restart: always
    image: ghcr.io/blakeblackshear/frigate:stable
    shm_size: "1024mb"  # Critical for multi-camera setups
    volumes:
      - /opt/frigate/config:/config
      - /opt/frigate/media:/media/frigate
      - /etc/localtime:/etc/localtime:ro
    ports:
      - "5000:5000"   # Web UI (unauthenticated)
      - "8971:8971"   # Web UI (authenticated, recommended)
    environment:
      - FRIGATE_RTSP_PASSWORD=your_secure_password
```

:::note[The shm_size Setting]
The `shm_size` setting allocates shared memory for video buffer handling. Multi-camera setups may need more—if you see "bus error" crashes, increase this to 2048mb or higher.
:::

### Step 3: Start and Get Your Password

```bash
docker compose up -d
docker logs frigate  # Note the auto-generated password for first login
```

### Step 4: Basic Configuration

Frigate's Web UI now includes a configuration editor, but let's start with a solid foundation:

```yaml
# /opt/frigate/config/config.yml

mqtt:
  enabled: false  # Enable if using Home Assistant

detectors:
  ov:
    type: openvino
    device: GPU  # Use Intel GPU

cameras:
  front_door:
    enabled: true
    ffmpeg:
      inputs:
        - path: rtsp://camera_ip:554/stream1
          roles:
            - record
        - path: rtsp://camera_ip:554/stream2
          roles:
            - detect
    detect:
      width: 1280
      height: 720
      fps: 5
    record:
      enabled: true
      retain:
        days: 14
        mode: motion
    objects:
      track:
        - person
        - car

  driveway:
    enabled: true
    ffmpeg:
      inputs:
        - path: rtsp://camera2_ip:554/stream1
          roles:
            - record
        - path: rtsp://camera2_ip:554/stream2
          roles:
            - detect
    detect:
      width: 1280
      height: 720
      fps: 5
    record:
      enabled: true
      retain:
        days: 14
        mode: motion
    objects:
      track:
        - person
        - car
```

This configuration:
- Uses Intel OpenVINO for detection (adjust for your hardware)
- Uses separate streams for detection (low-res, fast) and recording (high-res)
- Tracks only people and cars (ignore dogs, cats, birds)
- Keeps recordings for 14 days
- Only records when motion is detected (saves storage)

### Step 5: Proxmox LXC Installation

If you're running Frigate in a Proxmox LXC container (highly recommended for resource isolation), you'll need to passthrough hardware for acceleration.

**For Intel iGPU, add to `/etc/pve/lxc/<CTID>.conf`:**

```
lxc.cgroup2.devices.allow: c 226:0 rwm
lxc.cgroup2.devices.allow: c 226:128 rwm
lxc.mount.entry: /dev/dri dev/dri none bind,optional,create=dir 0 0
```

**For Coral TPU USB:**

```
lxc.cgroup2.devices.allow: c 189:* rwm
lxc.mount.entry: /dev/bus/usb dev/bus/usb none bind,optional,create=dir 0 0
```

**For Coral TPU PCIe:**

```
lxc.cgroup2.devices.allow: c 120:* rwm
lxc.mount.entry: /dev/apex_0 dev/apex_0 none bind,optional,create=file 0 0
```

## Achieving Zero False Alerts

This is where Frigate transforms from "cool technology" to "genuinely useful." The layered approach to false alert prevention is what sets it apart.

### Layer 1: Tune Motion Detection

Motion detection is your first filter—it triggers the AI to analyze a frame. If motion detection fires constantly, you're wasting CPU cycles.

**Use the Motion Tuner:** Frigate Web UI → Settings → Motion Tuner

- **Increase threshold** to reduce sensitivity to minor changes
- **Adjust contour area** to filter out small motion areas
- **Goal:** Motion triggers only for meaningful movement

### Layer 2: Apply Motion Masks

Motion masks tell Frigate to ignore static areas that constantly trigger motion—the swaying tree, the busy street in the background, the timestamp overlay.

```yaml
cameras:
  front_yard:
    motion:
      mask:
        - 0,0,1920,0,1920,100,0,100  # Top edge (sky)
        - 500,600,700,600,700,800,500,800  # That one bush
```

:::caution[Mask Purpose]
Motion masks affect motion detection, not object detection. Use them for areas that inherently move (trees, sky), not for areas where you don't want alerts (use zones and object masks for those).
:::

### Layer 3: Define Zones

Zones are where the magic happens. Instead of alerting on any object anywhere, you only alert when objects enter specific areas.

```yaml
cameras:
  front_door:
    zones:
      porch:
        coordinates: 500,720,800,720,800,400,500,400
        objects:
          - person
      driveway:
        coordinates: 0,720,400,720,400,300,0,300
        objects:
          - car
          - person
```

### Layer 4: Set Required Zones

This is the most powerful filter. When you set a `required_zones`, objects must enter that zone to generate alerts or recordings.

```yaml
cameras:
  front_door:
    required_zones:
      - porch
    zones:
      porch:
        coordinates: 500,720,800,720,800,400,500,400
        objects:
          - person
```

With this configuration:
- A person walking down the street? Ignored.
- A car driving past? Ignored.
- A person stepping onto your porch? **Alert.**

### Layer 5: Object Filters

Fine-tune detection sensitivity for specific object types:

```yaml
objects:
  track:
    - person
    - car
  filters:
    person:
      min_score: 0.65      # Minimum confidence to consider
      min_area: 1000       # Filter out tiny detections
      max_area: 100000     # Filter out impossibly large detections
      threshold: 0.7       # Confidence required to confirm
```

### The Optimization Workflow

1. **Start with defaults** and watch the debug view for a day
2. **Tune motion detection** until only meaningful motion triggers
3. **Add motion masks** for persistent false triggers
4. **Define zones** for areas you actually care about
5. **Set required_zones** to only alert on zone entry
6. **Fine-tune object filters** based on remaining false positives
7. **Add object masks** only for stubborn false positives in fixed locations

## Home Assistant Integration

Frigate's Home Assistant integration is where security becomes automation. When someone approaches your front door, your lights can turn on. When a car enters your driveway at 3 AM, you can get a notification with a snapshot.

### Installation

**Via HACS:**
1. Open HACS → Integrations
2. Search "Frigate" and click Download
3. Restart Home Assistant

**Configure the Integration:**
1. Settings → Devices & Services → Add Integration
2. Search "Frigate" and select it
3. Enter your Frigate URL (typically `http://frigate_ip:5000`)

### MQTT Configuration

For full functionality, Frigate needs to communicate with Home Assistant via MQTT:

```yaml
# /opt/frigate/config/config.yml
mqtt:
  enabled: true
  host: homeassistant_ip
  user: mqtt_username
  password: mqtt_password
```

### Entities Created

The integration automatically creates:

- **Camera entities** for live view
- **Binary sensors** for detected objects (sensor.front_door_person, etc.)
- **Sensors** for object counts
- **Event entities** for automation triggers

### Automation Examples

**Turn on lights when person detected at night:**

```yaml
automation:
  - alias: "Front Porch Light on Motion"
    trigger:
      - platform: state
        entity_id: binary_sensor.front_door_person
        to: "on"
    condition:
      - condition: state
        entity_id: sun.sun
        state: "below_horizon"
    action:
      - service: light.turn_on
        target:
          entity_id: light.front_porch
```

**Send notification with snapshot:**

```yaml
automation:
  - alias: "Person at Front Door"
    trigger:
      - platform: state
        entity_id: binary_sensor.front_door_person
        to: "on"
    action:
      - service: notify.mobile_app_your_phone
        data:
          title: "Person at Front Door"
          message: "Motion detected at the front door"
          data:
            image: /api/frigate/notifications/{{ states('sensor.front_door_last_person') }}/thumbnail.jpg
```

**Trigger alarm when person in restricted area:**

```yaml
automation:
  - alias: "Unauthorized Access Alert"
    trigger:
      - platform: state
        entity_id: binary_sensor.back_yard_person
        to: "on"
    condition:
      - condition: state
        entity_id: alarm_control_panel.home
        state: "armed_away"
    action:
      - service: alarm_control_panel.alarm_trigger
        target:
          entity_id: alarm_control_panel.home
```

## Camera Recommendations

Not all IP cameras are created equal. Your choice of camera affects Frigate performance, reliability, and security.

### What to Look For

**Must-Have Features:**
- **RTSP Streaming** — The only hard requirement for Frigate
- **Substream Support** — Critical for performance (low-res for detection, high-res for recording)
- **ONVIF Compliance** — Standard protocol for discovery and PTZ control
- **Local SD Card Recording** — Backup if your NVR goes offline

**Nice-to-Have Features:**
- H.264 encoding (better Frigate compatibility than H.265)
- PoE (Power over Ethernet) — One cable for power and data
- Wide dynamic range — Better low-light and backlight handling
- ONVIF Profile S and G — Full feature support

### Camera Brands That Work Well

**Reolink** — Excellent value, good RTSP implementation, wide selection. The 410/520/810/820 series substream support is solid. Avoid the battery/solar models for Frigate use.

**Amcrest** — Good budget options, reliable RTSP. The 4K models work well with Frigate's substream approach.

**Hikvision/Dahua** — Enterprise-grade cameras with excellent image quality. Note: Some models have had security vulnerabilities; keep firmware updated and isolate on VLANs.

**Ubiquiti UniFi Protect** — Great ecosystem, but RTSP requires the Protect controller. Works well if you're already in the UniFi ecosystem.

**Tapo/Eufy/TP-Link** — Budget consumer cameras. RTSP may be unofficial or unstable. Generally not recommended for serious setups.

### Camera Positioning Tips

- **Mount at 8-10 feet** — High enough to avoid tampering, low enough for clear faces
- **Angle downward 15-30°** — Capture faces, not the tops of heads
- **Avoid backlighting** — Don't point cameras toward windows or bright lights
- **Consider infrared range** — Budget cameras claim 100ft IR but deliver 30ft of usable range
- **Overlap coverage** — Ensure cameras cover each other's blind spots

## Storage: How Much Do You Need?

Video storage adds up fast. Let's do the math.

### Recording Mode Trade-offs

| Mode | Storage Use | Coverage | Best For |
|------|------------|----------|----------|
| 24/7 Continuous | Highest | Everything, all the time | Critical areas |
| Motion-Based | Medium | Only when motion detected | General use |
| Event-Based | Lowest | Only when AI detects objects | Storage-conscious setups |

### Storage Calculation Example

A single 4K camera recording 24/7 at 15 FPS with H.264 compression:

```
Bitrate: ~4 Mbps
Per day: 4 Mbps × 3600 × 24 / 8 = ~43 GB
Per week: ~300 GB
Per month: ~1.3 TB
```

With 4 cameras at 1080p, recording only on events (typically 2-4 hours/day per camera):

```
Per day: ~15-25 GB
Per month: ~500 GB
Per year: ~6 TB
```

### Storage Recommendations

**SSDs for Frigate database/cache:**
- Modern SSDs handle continuous writes well
- Faster UI and clip retrieval
- 500GB-1TB is sufficient for database + recent clips

**HDDs for long-term archives:**
- NVR-rated drives: WD Purple, Seagate SkyHawk
- Designed for 24/7 write workloads
- 4TB+ for reasonable retention

**Avoid:**
- Network storage (NFS/SMB) for live recording — Latency causes recording gaps
- Consumer desktop drives — Not rated for continuous writes

:::tip[Retention Strategy]
A hybrid approach works well: SSD (500GB-1TB) for Frigate database and recent events (last 14-30 days), then archive older clips to HDD if needed. Most incidents are discovered within days, so long-term retention is often unnecessary.
:::

## Troubleshooting Common Issues

### "Bus Error" Crashes

This indicates insufficient shared memory. Increase `shm_size` in your Docker Compose:

```yaml
services:
  frigate:
    shm_size: "2048mb"  # Increase from default 1024mb
```

### High CPU Usage

Several common causes:

1. **No substream configured** — Processing 4K video for detection instead of 720p
2. **Too high detection FPS** — 5 FPS is plenty for most use cases
3. **No hardware acceleration** — Ensure your detector is configured correctly

### Cameras Not Showing Up

Check RTSP connectivity first:

```bash
ffprobe rtsp://camera_ip:554/stream1
```

If this fails, the issue is with your camera, not Frigate.

### Detection Not Working

Verify your detector configuration matches your hardware:

```yaml
# For Intel OpenVINO
detectors:
  ov:
    type: openvino
    device: GPU

# For NVIDIA
detectors:
  tensorrt:
    type: tensorrt

# For Coral USB
detectors:
  coral:
    type: edgetpu
    device: usb
```

### Web UI Slow or Unresponsive

This often indicates database bloat. Check your recording retention settings:

```yaml
record:
  enabled: true
  retain:
    days: 14
    mode: motion  # Don't record 24/7 unless necessary
```

## Monitoring Your Frigate Installation

Like any critical homelab service, Frigate benefits from monitoring.

### Built-in Stats

Frigate exposes metrics at `/api/stats`:

- FPS for each camera
- Detection latency
- Camera connectivity status
- Storage usage

### Prometheus Integration

Frigate can export Prometheus metrics for integration with Grafana:

```yaml
# In config.yml
telemetry:
  enabled: true
  stats:
    export:
      prometheus:
        enabled: true
```

### Key Metrics to Watch

- **Detection FPS** — Should stay above your configured detection FPS
- **Detection latency** — Under 100ms is good; over 200ms needs investigation
- **Skipped frames** — Indicates CPU/GPU overload
- **Camera uptime** — Catch network issues early

### Alerting Rules

Set up alerts for:
- Camera offline for more than 5 minutes
- Detection latency above 200ms for more than 10 minutes
- Storage above 85% capacity
- Frigate container restart frequency

## When Things Go Wrong: Backup Strategies

Your NVR is only useful if it's recording. Plan for failures.

### Camera Redundancy

- **SD card recording** — Most cameras support local recording as backup
- **Power backup** — UPS (uninterruptible power supply) for cameras and NVR
- **Network redundancy** — Managed switch with PoE can alert on disconnected cameras

### Frigate Redundancy

For critical installations, consider:
- **Proxmox snapshots** — Quick rollback from configuration errors
- **Docker volume backups** — Regular backups of `/opt/frigate/config`
- **Secondary NVR** — Blue Iris or another NVR as hot standby

### Data Recovery

If your Frigate database gets corrupted:

1. Stop the container: `docker compose down`
2. Move the corrupt database: `mv /opt/frigate/media/frigate.db /opt/frigate/media/frigate.db.bak`
3. Start fresh: `docker compose up -d`
4. Note: This loses event history, but recordings on disk are preserved
